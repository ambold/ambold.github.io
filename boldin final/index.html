What is executive clemency?

Executive clemency, granted in Article II, Section 2 of the Constition, is the power of the President to grant relief to people convicted of federal charges. Executive clemency most often comes in the form of pardons or commutations. An executive pardon is an official epression of fogiveness from the President and restores civil rights removed as part of a conviction, such as restrictions of the right to vote and barriers to obtaining employment. A presidential commutation completely or partially reduces a sentence that is currently being served, but does not remove resulting restrictions to civil rights.

The 8 presidents from 1969 to 2017 have used their executive clemency powers differently...

Presidents Nixon, Ford, and Carter granted far more clemency petitions than the presidents that followed them. The average number of petitions granted per year did not return to the highest levels until Barack Obama's presidency.

But the number of petitions granted per year does not clearly relate to a president's political party.

There are both democratic and republican presidents among those who granted the highest and lowest numbers of petitions per year.

Looking at types of clemency petitions separately reveals further differences.

While Presidents Obama and Nixon granted similar numbers of petitions each year, the types of petitions they granted were nearly opposite. Over the course of these 8 presidents, the number of pardons granted per year decreased, but the number of commutations granted per year skyrocketed during President Obama's tenure.
